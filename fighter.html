Fighter Aircraft

Fighter aircraft are designed to control airspace. Control is established by using missiles and/or gatling guns. In the early days of fighter jets air combat, dog fighting, was conducted with the aircraft in close proximity of each other due to the limited range of the weapons.

Today, fighters can engage targets that are miles away and, even though the distance has increased, technology allows today's weapons to be more accurate and more deadly. Fighter jets can fly at speeds greater than the sound of speed (767 miles/hour) and typically more than twice the sound of speed.

Transport Aircraft

F-15 Eagle

The F-15 Eagle began production in 1972 and entered service in 1976. The F-15 is an air superiority aircraft that is undefeated in air-to-air combat. There are a number of version built starting with the F-15A and F-15B in 1972, the F-15C and F-15D began production in 1979, and the F-15J began production in 1981. Today the current version is the F-15E Strike Eagle which entered service in 1989. The F-15 is a two-seat aircraft with twin engines and armament that includes medium and short-range air-to-air missiles and gatlin gun. The F-15 is flown by the a handful of foreign countries and the U.S. Air Force.

F-16 Fighting Falcon

The F=16 Fighting Falcon began production in 1972 and entered service in 1980. The F-16 is a multi-role fighter used by more nations than any other fighter jet. There are many versions of the F-16 beginning with the F-16A, a single seat aircraft, and the F-16B, a two seat aircraft, in 1972, the F-16C and F-16D (single and two seat aircraft respectively, began production in 1984 and entered service in 1991, and the F-16D and F-16E which never was entered into service. The F-16 comes in either a single of two seat configuration with a single engine and armament that includes Sidewinder short-range air-to-air missiles and Sparrow medium-range air-to-air missiles. The F-16 can also carry air-to-ground missiles. The F-16 flown by a large number of foreign countries and the U.S. Air Force.

F/A-18 Hornet

The F/A-18 Hornet began production in 1980 and entered service in 1984. The F/A-18 is both a fighter jet and attack aircraft. The F/A-18 began with the F/A-18A and F/A-18B version in 1980 and later added the F/A-18C and F/A-18D in 1987. Today the current version is the F/A-18E/F Super Hornet. The Hornet and Super Hornet armament includes a rotary cannon, Sidewinder short-range air-to-air missiles, Sparrow medium-range air-to-air missiles, and a variety of AGM air-to-ground missiles. The F/A-18 is flown by the U.S. Navy and U.S. Marine Corps and performs the same roles as the F-15 for the U.S. Air Force.

F-22 Raptor

The F-22 Raptor is an air-superiority aircraft intended to replace the F-15. The F-22 began production in 1994 and entered service in 2009. The F-22 is the most advanced aircraft in the world today and is a stealth aircraft, meaning it has a minimal range signature and, therefore, difficult for enemy radar to detect the aircraft as it flies through the air. Many of the details about the F-22 are still classified and not available to the public. Today only the U.S. Air Force flies the F-22 due to the secrecy of the aircraft's avionic and stealth technology.

F-35 Lightning II

The F-35 is a multi-role aircraft intended to replace the U.S. Air Force's A-10 and F-16 and the U.S. Navy and U.S. Marine Corps' F/A-18. Versions of the F-35 are also being built for more than ten other countries. The F-35 began production in 2006 and entered service in 2011. The F-35 only trails the F-16 in the number of foreign countries flying the aircraft. The armament the F-35 can deploy is extensive and includes a missionized gun, short and medium-range air-to-air missiles, and air-to-ground missiles.
